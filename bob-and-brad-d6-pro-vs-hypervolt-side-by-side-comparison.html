Neuromuscular Junction — The neuromuscular junction is the specialized synapse where a motor neuron communicates with a skeletal muscle fiber, converting electrical nerve impulses into mechanical muscle contraction. When an action potential arrives at the motor nerve terminal, it triggers the release of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine, which crosses the synaptic cleft and binds to receptors on the muscle fiber membrane, initiating contraction. Dysfunctional neuromuscular transmission can contribute to muscle spasms, trigger points, and excessive muscle tension. Percussion therapy may influence neuromuscular function by stimulating sensory receptors that modulate motor neuron activity, potentially explaining the muscle relaxation effects observed after massage gun treatment.

Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness — Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) is the pain and stiffness felt in muscles several hours to days after unaccustomed or strenuous exercise. The condition results from microscopic damage to muscle fibers, particularly during eccentric (lengthening) contractions, and typically peaks between 24 and 72 hours after the exercise session. DOMS is accompanied by inflammation, temporary reduction in range of motion, and decreased force production capacity. Treatment approaches include active recovery, massage, cold water immersion, and percussive therapy, all of which aim to increase blood flow and reduce the inflammatory response.
