Reference

Trigger Point — A myofascial trigger point is a hyperirritable spot within a taut band of skeletal muscle that is painful on compression, stretch, overload, or contraction. First described in detail by Dr. Janet Travell in the mid-twentieth century, trigger points are associated with palpable nodules in the muscle fibers and can produce referred pain patterns that radiate to distant areas of the body. The pathophysiology involves localized contraction knots where muscle fibers remain in a shortened state due to excessive release of acetylcholine at the motor end plate. Treatment methods include manual pressure, dry needling, injection therapy, and percussive devices that deliver focused mechanical stimulation to the affected area.

Golgi Tendon Organ — The Golgi tendon organ is a proprioceptive sensory receptor located at the junction between skeletal muscle fibers and their associated tendon, first described by the Italian physician Camillo Golgi. These receptors are sensitive to changes in muscle tension and play a crucial role in the autogenic inhibition reflex, a protective mechanism that causes a muscle to relax when excessive tension is detected. When stimulated by percussion therapy, Golgi tendon organs can trigger this relaxation response, effectively reducing muscle tone and alleviating tightness. This neurological mechanism is one of the primary ways that massage guns achieve their muscle-relaxing effects, distinct from the purely mechanical action of breaking up adhesions.

To treat the shoulder with a massage gun, focus on the deltoid, trapezius, infraspinatus, and supraspinatus muscles using a round or flat attachment. Avoid direct contact with the acromion process and other bony landmarks of the shoulder joint.
